Shooting Over 85? Stop Grinding Drivers on the Range

The range session that feels the most productive is usually the exact one keeping you stuck.

By Cameron Tennant · July 2, 2026 · 5 min read

Walk onto any range at 6 p.m. and watch the golfers who shoot in the 90s. Most of them are doing the same thing: pulling driver, teeing it high, and swinging out of their shoes. Bomb after bomb. It feels like work. It's the most satisfying club in the bag.

And it's one of the biggest reasons their scores never move.

If you're stuck over 85, I want to save you a season of wasted practice. The problem usually isn't your swing. It's what you're choosing to practice — and the driver is the most seductive wrong answer in golf.

Why the driver feels like practice (and isn't)

A flushed driver is the best feeling in amateur golf. Instant feedback, a satisfying sound, a ball that sails. Your brain reads all that dopamine as "improvement." But feeling good and getting better are different things — and the driver is the club that lies to you most.

Here's the math nobody wants to hear: you hit driver maybe fourteen times a round. You hit wedges, chips, pitches, and putts forty, fifty, sometimes sixty times. Your practice should look like your scorecard — and your scorecard is not made of tee shots.

Where your strokes actually go at 85+

Go back through your last round honestly and tag where each stroke was lost. For almost every golfer over 85, the damage is in four places, and none of them is "I didn't hit it far enough off the tee":

  • Around the green. The chunked chip, the bladed pitch, the simple up-and-down you turned into a double.
  • Inside 100 yards. Wedge shots that should finish tight and instead leave you a 40-foot putt.
  • On the green. Three-putts — the cheapest strokes in golf to give away and the easiest to fix.
  • The blow-up hole. One penalty or one panicked decision that turns a bogey into a triple.

Add those up. Then add up the strokes you actually lost because your drive went 220 instead of 245. It's not close. The distance you're grinding for on the range is worth almost nothing until the scoring clubs stop bleeding shots.

The range trap

There's a second problem with the driver bucket: how you're practicing. Standing in one spot hitting the same club forty times is what coaches call block practice. It feels smooth and productive because you groove a rhythm — but the golf course never lets you hit the same shot twice. Real improvement comes from varied, uncomfortable reps that look like actual golf: different lies, different targets, one ball, full routine.

Fifty mindless drivers isn't practice. It's a warm-up you mistook for a workout.

What to hit instead

If you shoot in the 80s and 90s, here's where your time actually pays off — roughly in this order:

  • Short game around the green. The most trainable strokes in golf. Getting up-and-down 40% of the time instead of 20% finds you three or four shots a round without touching your full swing.
  • Wedges from 50–125 yards. Your scoring zone. Tighter proximity here turns scrambles into tap-ins. This is the number low-handicappers obsess over.
  • Putting — speed and the short ones. Kill the three-putt and drain everything inside six feet. Zero new talent required, immediate scores.
  • A reliable tee shot — not a longer one. Yes, keep the driver sharp enough to find the fairway. But "in play" beats "twenty more yards" every single time at this level. Practice a stock, repeatable tee ball you trust under pressure.

A simple rule: for every bucket of full swings, spend twice as long inside 100 yards and on the putting green. Your handicap will feel the difference long before your ego does.

The honest test

You don't have to take my word for it. Next round, track two numbers: strokes lost off the tee versus strokes lost from 100 yards and in. The data ends the argument every time — because feel is the least reliable thing in golf, and almost everyone is stunned by how much the short stuff is costing them.
