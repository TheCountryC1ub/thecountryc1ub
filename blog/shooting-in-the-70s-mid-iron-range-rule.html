Shooting in the 70s? Spend Only 10% of Your Range Time on Your Mid-Irons

This one's for the good players. Once you're breaking 80, the clubs you love to flush are the ones quietly wasting your practice.

By Cameron Tennant · July 3, 2026 · 5 min read

If you're shooting in the 70s, the goal has changed. You're not trying to break 90 anymore — you're chasing par. And at that level you've earned a different kind of problem, one almost nobody warns you about: you're probably pouring your range time into the clubs you're already great with.

Specifically, your 8-iron through your 4-iron. Here's the uncomfortable truth — you're not losing shots with those clubs. So they deserve about 10% of your practice. No more.

Your mid-irons aren't the problem

By the time you're a 70s player, your 4-through-8 irons are grooved. They're full, repeatable swings you've made ten thousand times. You flush a crisp 6-iron at the range flag, it feels incredible, and you hit forty more — because it's the best feeling in practice.

But go back through your last five rounds honestly and count the strokes those clubs actually cost you. For a player at your level, it's close to none. Those swings hold up under pressure. Hitting more of them is maintenance disguised as improvement — and it's stealing time from the shots that are genuinely keeping you from par.

The 90/10 range rule

Keep the mid and long irons sharp with roughly a tenth of your session — a handful of balls, each to a real target, full routine, just to confirm your strike and start line. That's the whole job. Then spend the other 90% where a scratch player quietly beats you.

Where a 70s player actually loses shots

  • Wedges and scoring distances (50–125 yards). This is the number one gap between good and great. The pro hits it to eight feet; you leave it thirty. That fraction of a stroke per hole is the whole difference between 74 and 69. Practice exact and in-between yardages, not stock full swings.
  • The short game under pressure. Not chip-and-runs off a perfect lie — awkward lies, short-sided pins, the up-and-down you have to convert to save the round.
  • Putting from makeable range. One loose three-putt or a missed six-footer is your round at this level. Drill speed and the short ones — the three-putt is the cheapest stroke you give away.
  • The tee ball — dispersion, not distance. A single penalty or a block into the trees ends a par round. Own a reliable one-way miss and you take the big number off the table.

What a smarter session looks like

Flip the ratio and a good player's range session looks completely different:

  • ~10% mid and long irons — a quick tune-up, then put them away.
  • The bulk on wedges at odd numbers — 37, 68, 91 yards — plus partial shots and trajectory control.
  • Real short-game reps from bad lies and tight pins.
  • Putting: a speed ladder from long range and a make-everything-inside-six-feet block.

Practice the shots you actually face on the course — not the ones that feel best on the mat.

Don't guess — know your leaks

Here's the catch: good players feel where they're losing shots, and feel is the least reliable thing in golf. The only way to allocate your practice correctly is to know your proximity by club and your strokes gained by category — in real numbers, not gut feel.
