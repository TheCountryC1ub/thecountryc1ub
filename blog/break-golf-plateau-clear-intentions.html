Stuck at the Same Score? How Clear Intentions Break a Golf Plateau

A plateau is rarely a talent problem. It's an intention problem, and the moment your goals go fuzzy, bad habits step in and lazy shots follow.

By Cameron Tennant · July 6, 2026 · 6 min read

Every golfer knows the feeling. You improved fast for a while. Then the number stopped moving. Same score in the spring, same score in the fall. You still play, you still practice, and nothing changes. It starts to feel like this is just your level.

Here's what I've learned, from college golf to grinding my own game back: a plateau is almost never a talent ceiling. It's a drift. Somewhere along the way you stopped playing with clear intentions, and golf punishes vagueness more than almost any game on earth.

Where lazy shots actually come from

Think about the last round that got away from you. I'd bet it wasn't the big miss that did the damage. It was the careless ones: the wedge you hit at "the green" instead of a number, the tee shot you stepped into still deciding between two clubs, the four-footer you walked up to and hit without a read because you were annoyed about the last hole.

None of those are swing flaws. They're intention failures. When you stand over a ball without a clear job for that shot, your brain doesn't go neutral — it goes lazy. Old habits step in to fill the vacuum: quick tempo, sloppy aim, no commitment. The swing you make is the swing your habits make, not the one you chose.

Multiply that by four or five shots a round and there's your plateau. You don't need a new swing. You need to stop giving strokes away to autopilot.

The big goal: know what you're actually playing for

Getting unstuck starts above the shot level. Ask yourself a question most golfers haven't answered honestly in years: what am I actually trying to do in this game? Break 80 for the first time. Get to single digits. Win the flight at your club championship. Beat your buddies with money on the line.

It doesn't matter how big or small the mission is. What matters is that it's specific and written down. Because the big goal is what everything else hangs from. When you know you're chasing single digits, a practice session has a purpose. A casual Tuesday nine has a purpose. Even a bad round has a purpose, because it's data on the way to something.

Without a mission, golf quietly becomes maintenance. You show up, you swing, you shoot your number. That's the plateau in a sentence.

The small goals: give every round a job

The big goal is too far away to swing at. You can't "break 80" on the 4th tee. So you translate it down into small goals, one or two per round, and make them things you control:

  • No doubles. When trouble shows up, take your medicine and get the ball back in play.
  • Full routine on every shot, even the tap-ins, even when the group is rushing you.
  • Commit to every club. If you're between numbers, decide, then swing like you mean it.
  • Two-putt speed on everything outside ten feet.

Notice what these have in common: you can succeed at all of them and still shoot a bad score. It will still have been a good round, because you did the things that lower scores over time. Small goals are how you win on days the golf gods don't cooperate. They keep you engaged on hole 14 when the card is already ugly, which is exactly when lazy golfers check out and grooved habits get worse.

The shot level: one ball, one intention

Then there's the smallest unit of the game, and the one that decides everything: the shot in front of you. Before every swing, you should be able to answer three questions out loud:

  • Where exactly is this ball going? A target the size of a bath towel, not a fairway.
  • How is it getting there? Shape, height, the miss you're playing away from.
  • Why this shot? What it sets up next: the number you want in, the side of the hole you want to putt from.

That's it. That's the whole discipline. It costs about ten seconds per shot, and it's the difference between playing golf and just hitting balls in a pretty place. When every shot has a declared job, there's no vacuum for the bad habits to fill. Commitment isn't a personality trait. It's a byproduct of clarity.

The feedback loop: how you know it's working

Here's the part almost everyone skips. Intentions without review fade in about two weeks. You'll set goals, feel great, and drift right back to autopilot. What makes it stick is closing the loop after the round: Did I hold my routine? Where did I get lazy? Which holes did I play with a plan, and which did I sleepwalk through?
